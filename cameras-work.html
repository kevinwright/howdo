How do cameras work?

Find out

All camera's - whether a film camera or digital camera - work by capturing light. They record the amount of light they capture and turn this information into an image.

  • Back
  • Next

Light reflects off objects in all directions. The job of the lens is to take that light and direct it onto the camera sensor at a single point, making an image sharp.

A lens is made of curved glass which directs the light onto the sensor or film. The glass in a lens can sometimes move. This allows the camera to get closer to a subject or further away.

  • Back
  • Next

When you take a photo, the camera shutter opens to let light onto the sensor. It then closes. The amount of time the shutter stays open depends on the amount of light the camera needs to make a picture.

It's a bit like opening the door from a dark room into a light room. Light comes into the dark while the door is open.

  • Back
  • Next

When light enters the camera through the lens, it is captured on a sensor. A sensor is made up of millions of pixels, little squares which each record how much light hits them and the colour of the light.

  • Back
  • Next

The light that hits each pixel is made up of the three primary colours. Red, blue and green. For example blue sky in a photo is made up of thousands of pixels picking up different shades of blue.

Each pixel records the light and colour and gives it a number. This number is a record of the amount of red, green and blue (RGB for short) for that single pixel. The RGB number for blue for example would be R=0, G=0, B=255.

  • Back
  • Next

The camera receives all this information from the sensor and shows the photo digitally back to you.

  • Back
  • Next

Film cameras work in a really similar way. But instead of a sensor capturing the the colours of light, a film which contains light sensitive crystals captures the chemical change to the crystals. It can then be developed into a photograph.
